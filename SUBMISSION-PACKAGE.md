### Abstract (250 words max for SSRN)
```
This memorandum analyzes the evolving legal landscape for challenging
"acqui-hire" transactions‚Äîacquisitions structured primarily to acquire
founders and key team members while leaving venture capital investors
and option holders with minimal recovery.

Drawing on systematic research of Delaware case law via the CourtListener
API, this analysis synthesizes recent Delaware Supreme Court decisions
that have reshaped fiduciary duty litigation in M&A contexts. Key cases
examined include Tornetta v. Musk (Tesla compensation litigation), In re
Mindbody, Inc. Stockholder Litigation, In re Columbia Pipeline Group
Merger Litigation, and In re Match Group Derivative Litigation.

The memo identifies five critical developments: (1) entire fairness
review remains applicable when founders/directors are on both sides of
transactions; (2) acquirer liability for aiding and abetting has been
substantially narrowed, now requiring actual knowledge of both the
breach and the wrongfulness of the acquirer's own conduct; (3) rescission
remedies may be unavailable post-consummation, requiring plaintiffs to
propose alternative remedies; (4) standing for derivative dilution claims
is lost upon merger following Brookfield; and (5) option holder claims
remain primarily contractual.

The analysis provides practitioners with a framework for evaluating
potential causes of action, including breach of fiduciary duty, Revlon
violations, fraudulent transfer claims, and contract-based theories.
Using the Tesla/xAI shareholder litigation as a contemporary example,
the memo illustrates how these legal standards apply to transactions
where controllers may extract value at the expense of minority
stakeholders.
```
